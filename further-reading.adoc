== Further Reading

The texts in this canon represent a narrow selection: the earliest recoverable teachings of Jesus, filtered for independence from Paul and proximity to the historical figure. Many other ancient texts were considered and excluded — not because they are worthless, but because they did not meet the criteria for this particular collection. Several are extraordinary works in their own right. What follows is a guide to the most important ones.

=== The Old Testament

The Hebrew Bible is the scripture Jesus himself read, quoted, and debated. The Psalms, the Prophets, and the Wisdom literature (Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, Sirach) form the background against which everything in this canon was written. Jesus's teaching about the Kingdom, about justice for the poor, and about the priority of mercy over sacrifice is rooted in the prophetic tradition of Amos, Micah, and Isaiah.

It is worth reading with one observation in mind: the God described in much of the Old Testament — a God who commands genocide, demands blood sacrifice, hardens hearts, and punishes children for the sins of their fathers — appears markedly different from the Father Jesus describes. The Father of the Sermon on the Mount sends rain on the just and the unjust alike, forgives without condition, and is found not in temples but in the inner room. Whether this reflects Jesus correcting, reinterpreting, or superseding the older tradition is a question this canon leaves to the reader.

_Not included because it is a separate canon in its own right, not a single text._

=== 1 Enoch (Book of Enoch)

1 Enoch is a composite Jewish apocalyptic text, parts of which date to the 3rd century BCE — making it older than much of the Old Testament. It is the source of the "Son of Man" tradition that Jesus appears to have drawn on, and its "Parables of Enoch" describe a pre-existent heavenly figure who will judge the wicked and vindicate the righteous. Its social justice material is fierce: "Woe to you who build your houses through the hard toil of others."

Jude, included in this canon, quotes 1 Enoch directly (1 Enoch 1:9), confirming that Jesus's own family regarded it as authoritative scripture. The Ethiopian Orthodox Church includes it in their biblical canon to this day.

_Not included because its cosmology describes the God of creation as the same figure who commands angels, destroys the wicked, and rules through fear — closer to the Demiurge than to the Father Jesus describes. Its importance to the tradition is undeniable, but its theology sits in tension with the core teaching of this canon._

=== The Gospel of Judas

Discovered in the 1970s in Egypt and published in 2006, the Gospel of Judas presents a radically different portrait of Jesus's betrayer. In this text, Jesus laughs at his disciples for praying to their God — implying they are worshipping the wrong deity entirely. He takes Judas aside and gives him secret teaching about the true divine realm, far above the creator of the material world. Judas's "betrayal" becomes an act of obedience: Jesus asks him to do it.

_Not included because of its late date (mid-2nd century at earliest), its fragmentary state, uncertainties in translation, and its distance from the earliest tradition. But the scene of Jesus laughing at temple worship resonates with Thomas and with the inner teaching of Mary — suggesting the Gospel of Judas may preserve, in distorted form, a genuine strand of the tradition._

=== Thunder, Perfect Mind

One of the most remarkable texts discovered at Nag Hammadi. It is a poem spoken by a divine feminine voice who identifies herself through paradox: "I am the first and the last. I am the honoured one and the scorned one. I am the whore and the holy one." The voice is simultaneously Sophia (Wisdom), the fallen divine feminine, and every woman.

_Not included because it contains no connection to Jesus or his teaching. It is included here because it represents the divine feminine voice that the orthodox tradition systematically suppressed — the same tradition that demoted Mary Magdalene from teacher to penitent prostitute. Thunder, Perfect Mind is what the feminine divine sounds like when she speaks for herself._

=== The Book of Thomas the Contender

A dialogue between Jesus and his "twin" Thomas, discovered at Nag Hammadi. It belongs to the same Thomasine tradition as the Gospel of Thomas but takes a sharply different direction: extreme asceticism, condemnation of the body, and warnings about the fires awaiting those who indulge physical desire.

_Not included because its harsh body-hatred contradicts the Letter from James ("every good gift and every perfect gift is from above"), the Didache's balanced ethic, and the overall tenor of this canon. Where the Gospel of Thomas says the Kingdom is spread upon the earth, the Book of Thomas the Contender says the earth is a prison to be escaped._

=== The Acts of Thomas

A 3rd-century narrative of the apostle Thomas's missionary journey to India, containing miracles, conversions, and talking animals. Its theological framework is Syriac Christianity — the eastern tradition that preserved the Thomasine sayings.

The Hymn of the Pearl, extracted as an appendix to this canon, is embedded within the Acts of Thomas but is almost certainly older than the narrative frame.

_Not included (apart from the Hymn) because the miracle saturation and narrative embellishment place it far from the earliest teaching tradition. The Acts of Thomas is a 3rd-century romance, not a 1st-century teaching text._

=== The Gospel of Truth

A Valentinian meditation on salvation, possibly written by Valentinus himself (mid-2nd century). It reads like a prose poem and draws on Pauline language — particularly the imagery of being "called by name" and the metaphor of anointing. It describes the Father as unknowable, the Son as revelation, and ignorance as the root of suffering.

_Not included because of its Pauline connections and its Valentinian theological framework, which postdates and reinterprets the earliest tradition. But it is beautifully written and represents the most sophisticated attempt to reconcile Gnostic insight with the emerging Christian mainstream._

=== The Apocryphon of John (Secret Book of John)

The most important Sethian Gnostic text, found in multiple copies at Nag Hammadi. It provides the fullest surviving account of Gnostic cosmology: how the unknowable Father emanated the divine realms, how Sophia's error produced the Demiurge (Yaldabaoth), and how the Demiurge created the material world in ignorance, believing himself to be the only God.

_Not included because it is a cosmological treatise, not a teaching of Jesus — although it frames itself as a post-resurrection revelation to John. Its value is explanatory: if you want to understand what the Gospel of Thomas means by "the All," what the Gospel of Mary means by the soul's ascent past the powers, or why Jesus in the Gospel of Judas laughs at temple worship, the Apocryphon of John provides the underlying map._

=== The Dead Sea Scrolls (Community Rule)

The Community Rule (1QS) is one of the most important Dead Sea Scrolls, describing the practices of the Essene community at Qumran in the 1st century BCE. It contains a "Two Ways" teaching (the Way of Light and the Way of Darkness) strikingly similar to the Didache's "Two Ways" (the Way of Life and the Way of Death), suggesting a shared tradition.

_Not included because it is pre-Christian and contains no Jesus teaching. Its importance is contextual: it demonstrates that the ethical framework of the Didache — and by extension, of Jesus's teaching — did not emerge in a vacuum but from a living tradition of Jewish sectarian practice._

=== The Book of Jubilees

A Jewish text from the 2nd century BCE that retells Genesis and Exodus with an emphasis on calendar observance, purity laws, and strict legal obedience. It was influential in the Enochic tradition and is canonical in the Ethiopian Orthodox Church alongside 1 Enoch.

_Not included because it is pre-Christian and its legalistic framework runs counter to the spirit of this canon. Where Jesus says the sabbath was made for humanity, Jubilees says violation of the sabbath is punishable by death._

=== The Gospel of the Hebrews / Gospel of the Ebionites / Gospel of the Nazarenes

Three fragmentary Jewish-Christian gospels, known only from quotations in the Church Fathers. They appear to represent the gospel tradition of James's Jerusalem community — the very community closest to the historical Jesus. The Gospel of the Hebrews describes the Holy Spirit as Jesus's mother; the Gospel of the Ebionites is vegetarian; the Gospel of the Nazarenes closely parallels Matthew but with significant variations.

_Not included because they survive only in fragments quoted by hostile sources (Epiphanius, Jerome, Origen), making reliable reconstruction impossible. If any of these texts survived intact, they would almost certainly belong in this canon. Their loss is among the most consequential in Christian history._

=== The Mandaean Ginza Rba

The holy book of the Mandaeans, a living Gnostic religion that reveres John the Baptist as the true prophet and regards Jesus as a false messiah. The Ginza Rba ("Great Treasure") contains cosmological material, hymns, and ritual instructions. The Mandaeans practise repeated baptism, which they trace to John.

_Not included because it belongs to a separate religious tradition. Its value is as a witness to the Baptist movement that preceded and influenced Jesus. If the Mandaean tradition preserves authentic memory of John's teaching, it provides a window into the world Jesus entered when he went to the Jordan to be baptised._
